Despite a rocky start to Q2, with the weekly average for compound annual growth temporarily dipping into the negatives before rebounding; our B2B MRR Index had a promising quarter as it hit an all-time high of 4265 by the end of June.

This steady climb in recurring revenue for companies tracked in our B2B MRR index was driven by a strong compound annual growth rate (CAGR) in the last two months of the quarter.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B CAGR Monthly](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEMFGsbswqTcGl_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BCAGRMonthly.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

  
This nascent ‚Äústeady state‚Äù for CAGR is noteworthy, as it‚Äôs significantly higher than the average growth rates B2B saw over the last 3 quarters, where growth hit a plateau of 3-5%, with frequent dips into the negatives.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B CAGR Quarterly](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEMVGsbswqTcGm_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BCAGRQuarterly.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

This meant that Q2 saw an overall CAGR of 5.1% ‚Äì far surpassing Q1‚Äôs measly 1.4%, while posting the highest quarterly growth rate seen since Q2 of 2024.

With CAGR remaining stable at 10% for 10 consecutive weeks, **we have likely seen B2B growth rates settle at a ‚Äúnew normal‚Äù,** after 12 months of sluggish growth (averaging 1-3%) punctuated by frequent dips and fluctuations.

If this is indeed the case, we can expect the rest of 2025 to be excellent for B2B ‚Äì with recurring revenues climbing steadily, and our B2B MRR Index reaching a record value of 4450 by the end of 2025.

## Strong sales and low churn drive B2B results

So what‚Äôs driving this new-found B2B growth? Let‚Äôs break recurring revenue into its components - new sales, churn, upgrades & downgrades - and explore what‚Äôs going on.

Starting with the Profitwell New Sales Index, we find strong and steady sales throughout the quarter, with the index hovering at a baseline of 1.343 for Q2 ‚Äì 5.4% higher than last quarter, and showing significant gains compared to New Sales Index values of 1.274, 1.172, and 1.307 for Q3 ‚Äò24, Q4 ‚Äò24, and Q1 ‚Äò25 respectively.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B New Sales](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEL1GsbswqTcGk_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BNewSales.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

  
The B2B New Sales Index closely maps to the trajectory of our earlier B2B CAGR graph - 12 months of sluggish growth starting in mid-2024, that then settled at a new, slightly higher ‚Äústeady state‚Äù this quarter - indicating that growing was one of the key factors in this quarter‚Äôs B2B rebound.

The sustained jump in sales was simultaneously buttressed by a drop in churn rates from May through June, after a rocky start to the quarter. Q2 opened with an average Churn Index value of -1.638 (ie, a churn rate that is 1.638x the churn rate seen when the index was created in 2019), before dropping to -1.545 and -1.507 in May and June.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B Churn](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEMlGsbswqTcGn_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BChurn.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

The good news doesn‚Äôt end there either. This quarter's overall average for churn levelled out to -1.563; the lowest we‚Äôve seen since the end of 2022.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B Churn Quarterly](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEM1GsbswqTcGo_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BChurnQuarterly.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

While it‚Äôs difficult to say whether churn will continue dropping to new lows, or start to level off over the next few quarters, we can safely expect diminished churn to drive further B2B gains for the rest of 2025.

Although downgrades (and upgrades) typically have a much smaller impact on recurring revenue than new sales and churn, the Profitwell Downgrade Index‚Äôs performance was also a cause for celebration.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2B Downgrades](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlELVGsbswqTcGi_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2BDowngrades.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

It followed a similar pattern to the New Sales Index, dropping by 13% to a quarterly average value of -2.416 compared to Q1‚Äôs average of -2.736. This brings to an end 3 consecutive quarters of elevated downgrades, improving B2B's growth prospects for 2025.

Finally, looking at the Upgrade Index, we find a 2% drop to an average of 2.675 compared to Q1‚Äôs average of 2.736. While this has a very minor impact on B2B growth, we‚Äôll be watching closely in Q3 to see if upgrades continue to fall, and start impacting B2B growth.

## A return to calm, and early AI wins

While the market comes to terms with the new reality of [~$3T in US tariffs](https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-july-14-2025) (and the initial panic dissipates), we‚Äôre starting to see SaaS businesses ‚Äúexhale‚Äù after holding their collective breaths and bracing for the worst following Q1‚Äôs tariff announcements.

This is likely what drove Q2‚Äôs rebound in sales and drop in churn, as businesses that had laid off workers, paused purchases, and cut non-critical expenses adjusted to a trade environment that wasn‚Äôt as bad as anticipated, and resumed investments in tooling, R&D and growth.

It‚Äôs also worth noting that the majority of SaaS businesses aren‚Äôt directly impacted by the current tariff regime ‚Äì instead facing secondary effects as their customers adjust to the impact on their operations and supply chains.

However, this alone cannot explain the 10 consecutive weeks of strong revenue growth we observed this quarter. While we can‚Äôt directly confirm this with Profitwell SaaS Index data, we strongly suspect that Q2‚Äôs growth is driven by the introduction of AI into existing B2B SaaS products.

We see the recent [flurry of acquisitions](https://news.crunchbase.com/ma/ai-enterprise-startup-ma-higher-h1-2025-data/), and [meteoric rise](https://analyticsindiamag.com/ai-features/cursor-is-the-fastest-growing-saas-in-history/) of several nascent, AI-first SaaS companies as the leading indicators of revenue growth and retention improvements at B2B SaaS companies that have integrated AI into their core product offerings.

In our [previous report](https://www.paddle.com/blog/saas-market-report-q1-2025), we spoke about B2B SaaS companies re-configuring their organizations to better integrate AI with their existing product offerings. We are now seeing the early dividends of these changes, as product teams learn to solve customer needs with AI; and sales teams start to sell & price these solutions effectively.

Combining the lift in revenue from early AI wins, and an [anticipated interest rate cut](https://www.reuters.com/business/goldman-sachs-expects-fed-deliver-three-rate-cuts-2025-2025-07-01/) by the US Fed in Q3, we forecast that **B2B growth will remain strong for the rest of 2025** \- maintaining or even surpassing the 10% CAGR that we saw in the last 10 weeks of the quarter.

## B2C climbs despite anticipated summer slowdown

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C MRR](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlENFGsbswqTcGp_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CMRR.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

Echoing the trajectory of B2B growth, the companies composing our B2C SaaS Index had a difficult start to Q2. Over the first week of March, growth temporarily plunged to -30% CAGR, dropping recurring revenues (as measured by the Profitwell MRR Index) to a 9-week low of 2074.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C CAGR Monthly](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlENVGsbswqTcGq_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CCAGRMonthly.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

However, before the dip could do serious harm to B2C revenue, CAGR quickly rebounded, stabilising at a higher average than we saw in Q1. In fact, in May and June, B2C CAGR reached 8.0% and 5.3% respectively, propelling our MRR Index to an all-time high of 2115 (ie, 2.115x the index‚Äôs recurring revenue in 2019, when it was created).

Like B2B, this indicates that the slump in Q1 - driven by uncertainty and panic about the tariffs - is over. Don‚Äôt celebrate too quickly though. With summer starting, we anticipate a seasonal drop in SaaS spending, as consumers prioritize travel and in-person experiences over software spending. The effects of this are already apparent, as evidenced by CAGR‚Äôs 33% drop between May and June.

## New summer; same slow sales

To explain a bit further, we can again examine the four components of subscription revenue growth - new sales, churn, upgrades and downgrades (the last two typically having a much smaller impact).

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C New Sales](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlENlGsbswqTcGr_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CNewSales.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

Starting with sales, we see strong and steady growth for the first half of the quarter, with the New Sales Index averaging 1.046, before dropping 10.5% to 0.936 in the last half of the quarter - likely driven by the [summer slowdown](https://www.paddle.com/blog/saas-market-report-july-2024) in consumer spending.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C Churn](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEN1GsbswqTcGs_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CChurn.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

Simultaneously, churn fell consistently throughout the quarter - dropping 4.7%, from -1.241 in April, to -1.183 by June. Additionally, we observed that the periodic churn spikes typical to this index became much smaller - potentially driven by consumers interacting with their software less (and thus forgetting to cancel), as they took time off for the summer.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C Downgrades](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEOFGsbswqTcGt_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CDowngrades.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

Downgrades also drove Q2‚Äôs steady B2C growth, albeit with a much smaller impact than new sales and churn. After months of higher-than-average downgrades (averaging -1.911 in Q1), the Downgrade Index fell to -1.569 and -1.677 in May and June respectively.

![SaaS Index - Q2 25 - B2C Upgrades](https://images.prismic.io/paddle/aIlEOVGsbswqTcGu_SaaSIndex-Q225-B2CUpgrades.jpeg?auto=format%2Ccompress&fit=max&w=3840)

Finally, with the Upgrade Index, we observed a stable average of 1.907 over Q2 - a 13.7% drop from the previous quarter‚Äôs average of 2.209. While fairly large, this drop in upgrades likely had a relatively small impact on total B2C CAGR. Nonetheless, the index is worth monitoring through Q3, for signs of a continuing drop in upgrades.

## AI bonanza or summer slowdown?

With tailwinds from new AI products and new AI features; and headwinds from summer dips in consumer software spending, what‚Äôs the outlook for B2C in 2025?

First, like we‚Äôve seen in B2B, it appears that market uncertainty and consumer confidence have returned to normal (likely driving B2C‚Äôs recovery) - as evidenced by external indicators such as the S&P 500‚Äôs Q2 rebound from its post-tariff Q1 losses.

With the summer slowdown in consumer SaaS spend typically beginning in May, and the overall decrease in CAGR typically exceeding 80%, this year‚Äôs 33% drop in CAGR between May and June is unusually small.

While we may simply be seeing a _delayed_ slowdown in spending, we suspect that an explosion of new AI products, and the implementation of AI in existing consumer products is pushing back against this downward pressure on B2C growth. Like we saw in B2B SaaS, the maturing ability to use AI in consumer products, while pricing and marketing them effectively, appears to be driving new gains in sales and revenue retention.

In the near-term, we can expect the summer dips in consumer software spending to dominate, and depress B2C growth in the first half of Q3. However, as consumers return to their regular spending habits in the fall, **we expect the surge in AI products to precipitate a significant rebound** , and drive significant growth in B2C through the end of 2025.

## Find the margin to free your AI

It‚Äôs no secret that running AI models is expensive. While AI unlocks an untold number of new consumer AI products, if you‚Äôre scaling a consumer app, running millions of LLM calls for your user base while handling performance marketing costs, platform fees, and a saturated app market could bring disaster to your finances.

That‚Äôs why it‚Äôs become critically important for AI consumer apps to grow their margin wherever they can. One recent development (and enormous advantage) for app developers with large US audiences is the ability to [re-direct users to external web checkouts](https://www.paddle.com/blog/apple-vs-epic-app-store-changes) for in-app purchases.

Not only does this give developers the power to **increase their revenue by up to 25%** on every purchase by using a third party payment provider (and avoiding Apple‚Äôs 30% commission), it also significantly improves cash flow by improving payout frequency and beating Apple's 30+ day standard.

For those with audiences outside the US - you have options too. Leading consumer apps like Noom and Flo, have perfected the art of web-first acquisition flows - converting users to paying customers before they enter their app, and avoiding Apple‚Äôs 30% commission completely. In addition, you gain the benefits of an expanded audience (web and app audiences only have a 15% overlap), and improved attribution,**** without the restrictions of SKAN and ATT.

These monetization strategies don‚Äôt come without additional responsibilities though. While the App Store charges a high commission, it also handles the complexity of international payments - fraud prevention, chargebacks, international tax remittance, and more. Most third-party PSPs leave this burden to you - which is why we advocate for working with a Merchant of Record (MoR) when you start moving payment volume to the web. These providers specialize in handling these payment complexities - giving developers an **‚Äúapp store like‚Äù payments experience, for far cheaper.**
